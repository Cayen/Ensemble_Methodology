# TERMINOLOGY
## Ensemble Methodology Standard Definitions

Version: 2.0

---

# Vector

A **Vector** is a major domain of analysis representing a system whose changes may influence other systems.

Vectors are independent for observation but interconnected through transmission mechanisms.

Examples:
- Energy
- Agriculture
- AI Infrastructure
- Manufacturing
- Financial Markets

A vector is **not** a topic.
It is an analytical domain.

---

# Gate

A **Gate** is a required verification checkpoint that information must pass before advancing to the next analytical state.

A gate exists to reduce analytical error by preventing premature conclusions.

Example:

Rumor

↓

Sourced

↓

Verified

↓

Integrated

↓

Operational

---

# Save State

A **Save State** is a formal archival snapshot of the methodology and analytical picture at a specific point in time.

A Save State preserves:

- Current assumptions
- Active vectors
- WATCHLIST
- Trigger matrix
- Corrections
- Data quality
- Methodology version

Purpose:

Allow future work to resume without relying on conversation history.

---

# Operational

**Operational** means verified information is actively influencing the current analytical framework.

Operational information:

- affects active vectors
- changes monitoring priorities
- appears in executive briefings
- informs future analysis

Operational does **not** mean permanent.

Operational status may be downgraded if later evidence contradicts it.

---

# Leading Indicator

A **Leading Indicator** is an observation that suggests a future development before the primary outcome occurs.

Examples:

- ENSO forecasts
- Policy announcements
- Capital expenditure plans
- Permit applications

Risk:

Leading indicators are early signals, not confirmation.

---

# State Indicator

A **State Indicator** describes the current condition of a system.

Examples:

- Active ceasefire
- Power grid emergency
- Signed agreement
- Hospitalization
- Existing sanctions

State indicators answer:

"What is true right now?"

---

# Lagging Indicator

A **Lagging Indicator** confirms an outcome that has already occurred.

Examples:

- USDA production reports
- Quarterly earnings
- Factory closures
- Unemployment statistics
- GDP releases

Risk:

Lagging indicators often confirm changes already underway.

---

# Layer R

**Layer R** (Reality Layer) represents the Ensemble's structured synthesis of verified observations into explanatory mechanisms.

Layer R contains analytical constructs rather than raw evidence.

Two classifications exist:

**ENSEMBLE-SYNTHESIS**

Multiple verified observations combined into a supported explanation.

Example:

"Manufacturers are absorbing costs through four adaptation mechanisms."

**ENSEMBLE-DEFINED**

Framework-specific terminology created by the Ensemble.

Example:

"Razor's Edge Viability"

Layer R must never be presented as primary evidence.

---

# Disputer

The **Disputer** is the Ensemble's adversarial reviewer.

Responsibilities:

- Challenge assumptions
- Search for falsification
- Identify alternative explanations
- Detect analytical drift
- Prevent confirmation bias

The Disputer critiques methodology rather than defending conclusions.

---

# Compiler

The **Compiler** is the human coordinator responsible for assembling field reports before analysis begins.

Responsibilities:

- Collect source material
- Preserve attribution
- Separate observation from interpretation
- Flag disputed information
- Initiate Gate verification

The Compiler does not perform synthesis during data collection.

---

# Promotion

**Promotion** is the advancement of information from one analytical state to another after satisfying the required gate.

Example:

SOURCED

↓

VERIFIED

↓

INTEGRATED

↓

OPERATIONAL

Promotion is evidence-driven.

Information is never promoted because it appears persuasive.

---

# Falsification

**Falsification** is the process by which evidence demonstrates that a hypothesis, mechanism, or analytical assumption is no longer supported.

Falsification removes or revises analytical assumptions.

It is considered a successful outcome of the methodology, not a failure.

---

# Resolution

**Resolution** occurs when uncertainty has been sufficiently reduced that active monitoring is no longer required.

Resolution does not necessarily imply success or failure.

Examples:

- Peace agreement signed
- Investigation completed
- Official medical statement released

---

# Superseded

A **Superseded** item has been replaced by a newer framework, event, or analytical model.

The original analysis is archived rather than deleted.

Purpose:

Preserve institutional memory while preventing obsolete assumptions from remaining operational.

---

# WATCHLIST

The **WATCHLIST** is the Ensemble's structured monitoring system for unresolved developments.

Every WATCHLIST item requires:

- Entry Date
- Current Status
- Temporal Classification
- Resolution Trigger
- Falsification Trigger
- Maximum Age
- Source Status

WATCHLIST items remain outside the operational model until their promotion criteria are satisfied.

The WATCHLIST prevents uncertainty from being forgotten while ensuring unresolved issues do not become operational assumptions.

Transmission Mechanism
The causal pathway by which change in one vector produces measurable effects in another vector.
AI Infrastructure

↓

Electricity Demand

↓

Grid Stress

↓

Industrial Electricity Prices

↓

Manufacturing Costs

Institutional Memory
The accumulated body of documented methodology, corrections, lessons learned, save states, and historical decisions that allows the Ensemble to maintain continuity independent of any individual model or conversation.
